01 — Vision & Mission

The coordination layer
for Africa's food system.

Not warehouses first. Better networks first. Infrastructure is an enabler — not the starting point.

Thesis updated: 2026-06-15
Status: Core
Africa doesn't just have a food production problem — it has a food movement problem. Consumers pay too much. Farmers earn too little. Food is lost through fragmentation, poor coordination, and inefficient movement. Every intermediary prices in uncertainty, spoilage, and risk. The result is a system where everyone loses value. TRACE exists to fix the coordination, not just add the warehouses.
The mission

What TRACE does

The operating mission

Reduce the cost and waste of moving food by coordinating the flow of products, information, and infrastructure across the food supply chain. Move food faster, track it better, waste less of it.

Not: Build warehouses and fill them. Yes: Build a better network and let the data tell you what to build next.

The thesis inversion

Why coordination comes before infrastructure

Traditional thinking: More warehouses → Less waste.

TRACE thinking: Better coordination → Better logistics → Less waste → Lower costs → Smarter infrastructure investment.

Infrastructure is a response to observed bottlenecks. Not an assumption about where they are.

The impact

Who benefits and how

Consumers: More affordable food, better quality, longer shelf life, greater consistency.

Farmers: Better market access, more predictable demand, higher realized incomes, lower rejection rates.

The food system: Less waste, smarter infrastructure, more resilient supply chains.

Economic philosophy
01
Pass efficiency gains to customers through lower prices.
When better coordination, higher throughput, or lower logistics costs reduce what it costs to move food, a meaningful share of that saving passes to the customer — not to the margin. TRACE wins by making food more affordable, not by charging more for it.
02
Retain sufficient margin to reinvest in technology, infrastructure, and expansion.
The efficiency gains are not given away entirely. Retained margin funds the intelligence layer, the next node, and eventually the infrastructure that makes the network more valuable. Sustainable reinvestment is the mechanism by which Phase 0 becomes Phase 2.
03
Win through higher throughput, better inventory turns, and operational excellence — not premium pricing.
TRACE is not a premium brand. It is an efficient operator. The competitive advantage is a lower cost to serve the same customer at the same or better quality — driven by volume, route density, supplier discipline, and accumulated operational data. This is a fundamentally different business model from charging more for traceable or organic produce.

02 — The Opportunity

Food is expensive
because coordination is expensive.

Africa does not simply need more cold rooms. It needs a more efficient path between farmers and demand. TRACE reduces cost and waste by improving coordination, logistics, and decision-making — then deploys physical infrastructure where data proves it creates durable value.

Updated: 2026-06-15
Status: Core
Food takes nearly 60% of household income in Nigeria. In 2024, food inflation hit 35.4% — an all-time high. The consequence is not simply that families eat less well. When food becomes more expensive, children leave school, healthcare is deferred, and future earnings are compressed. The food system's inefficiency is not an abstract economic problem. It is a direct constraint on human development. TRACE starts by asking: how much of this cost is coordination failure — and how much of it can be removed?
The coordination failure

Food loses value at every handoff. Farmers lack predictable demand. Buyers lack reliable supply. Consumers absorb the cost of spoilage, inefficiency, and uncertainty. Every participant prices in risk because the system lacks visibility and coordination.

Fragmented supply

Farmers without reliable demand signals

Smallholder farmers produce without knowing who will buy, at what price, or at what quality standard. The absence of forward demand forces short-term, reactive decision-making that compounds waste and reduces farmer income.

Fragmented demand

Buyers sourcing through opaque, inconsistent channels

Institutional buyers — restaurants, hotels, processors — source through informal channels with no quality standards, no price transparency, and no reliability guarantees. Uncertainty is the norm, not the exception.

Inefficient movement

Too many hands between farm and demand

Produce passes through aggregators, wholesalers, and traders — each pricing in their own uncertainty and handling risk. Every additional handoff adds time, cost, and spoilage. The consumer pays for all of it.

Waste priced in

Spoilage and uncertainty passed downstream

Because no participant can predict spoilage, demand, or price, every participant buffers with margin. Waste is not absorbed by the supply chain — it is systematically passed to the end consumer as higher prices.

Human capital at stake

Food affordability is a development crisis

Food inflation erodes real income for low-income households disproportionately. Research links food price shocks directly to reduced education spending, child nutrition gaps, stunted growth, and compressed future earnings. This is not a market friction — it is a development emergency.

A solvable problem

Better coordination, not just more storage

Much of the value lost in Africa's food system is not a function of insufficient infrastructure. It is a function of poor coordination, insufficient information, and inefficient movement. That is a logistics and data problem before it is a capital problem.

Where TRACE competes
Lower effective cost per usable kg
TRACE will not compete on having cheaper transport. It will compete by losing less. Better grading, faster movement, and demand-led purchasing convert higher logistics spend into lower effective cost.
Reliable supply — predictable quantity and quality
B2B customers — hotels, restaurants, schools, caterers — may value reliable weekly delivery more than the lowest headline price. Fewer stockouts, fewer market trips, fewer rejected deliveries is measurable value.
Grade-based revenue from every batch
Traditional markets mix quality levels and sell at one blended price. TRACE routes Grade A to premium channels, Grade B to general B2B, and safe surplus to discount or processing — extracting more total value from the same harvest.
Processing and waste monetisation
Instead of fresh sale or total loss, TRACE creates: premium fresh sale + standard fresh sale + processing recovery. Lower-grade, excess, or near-spoilage produce redirected into cut veg, frozen packs, or processor supply — multiple revenue paths from one batch.

Build the network first. Build infrastructure second.

TRACE starts by coordinating supply and demand through direct sourcing, quality standards, logistics, and operational data. Infrastructure becomes a targeted response to proven bottlenecks — not the first assumption.

The flywheel compounds with scale — every transaction makes the next one cheaper, faster, and less wasteful

01 — Demand Network

Build recurring demand before building assets

Develop a base of recurring B2B customers — restaurants, QSRs, hotels, caterers — who need reliable, graded, consistently supplied produce. Retail complements B2B by improving inventory utilization, generating consumer demand insights, and accelerating learning around pricing, quality, and purchasing behavior.

The thesis: Demand density lowers unit costs and makes infrastructure investable. No hub gets built without recurring demand that justifies the utilisation. LOIs come after demonstrated reliability, not before.

02 — Trusted Supply Network

Verified farmer relationships and supplier scorecards

Build direct relationships with an initial network of strategically selected farms across priority corridors. Grade every lot. Score every supplier. Know which farms deliver quality consistently, which are improving, and which need intervention before a large order is placed.

The thesis: The long-term moat is not farmer access alone — it is knowing which suppliers deliver quality reliably. That knowledge is built through volume and data, not through contracts alone.

03 — Efficient Movement

Route optimization, consolidation, harvest-to-demand timing

Optimize routing, consolidate farm collection and B2B delivery runs, and reduce harvest-to-customer time. Track cost per kg on every route. Remove the days and hands that create spoilage and cost without creating value.

The thesis: Faster, better-coordinated movement can create more value than simply storing food longer. A tomato that reaches a customer in 3 days needs no cold room. The same tomato sitting at a wholesale market for 7 days needs everything.

04 — Intelligence Layer

Data across harvest, grade, route, inventory, price, spoilage

Capture operational data on every lot, every route, every transaction, and every spoilage event. Build supplier scorecards, demand forecasting, route optimization, and quality tracking that compound over time.

The thesis: Every kilogram moved makes TRACE smarter. The intelligence layer compounds with every transaction. Better data leads to better sourcing, forecasting, routing, pricing, and infrastructure decisions — creating a durable advantage that is difficult to replicate.

05 — Targeted Infrastructure

Physical assets only where data proves economic need

Deploy aggregation centres, cold rooms, processing capabilities, and renewable-powered facilities only when operational data confirms a specific, measurable bottleneck that physical infrastructure would remove.

The thesis: Infrastructure should follow utilisation, not precede it. A cold room built on proven demand and route data is a different investment from a cold room built on a market size estimate.
Capabilities — important, but not the starting point

Cold storage, renewable energy, traceability, processing, and export readiness are genuine capabilities that TRACE will build toward. They are valuable after TRACE has proven demand density, supplier reliability, route economics, and quality improvement. They are not where we start.

Cold storage

  • Deployed where loss-stage data confirms storage — not transit — is the primary spoilage bottleneck
  • Sized from actual demand committed by B2B clients, not modelled utilisation
  • Currently Phase 2 planning · 24–36 months from Seasons launch

Clean energy

  • Deployed where it lowers operating cost and improves hub reliability at scale
  • A structural FX hedge — zero marginal energy cost when naira weakens
  • Part of the Phase 2 hub design · not a Phase 0 capital requirement

Traceability

  • Generated through operations from Day 1 — lot tracking, grade records, temperature logs
  • Not sold as the first product — built as the data layer that compounds with network density
  • Becomes a compliance and export tool when domestic operations are proven

Exports

  • Activated after domestic reliability and grading discipline are demonstrated
  • EUDR compliance and international market access follow supply chain proof — not precede it
  • USD-denominated revenue is a long-term hedge, not a Phase 0 revenue line

Processing

  • Added where recurring Grade C volumes and frozen B2B demand create value-capture opportunities
  • The Seasons frozen B2B channel is the first test — blast freeze investment follows proven demand
  • Processing at hub scale is a Phase 2 capability

Pharmaceutical cold chain

  • GDP-compliant pharma zone is part of the hub Phase 2 design
  • Premium pricing justified — NAFDAC compliance and excursion audit trail add real value
  • Requires regulatory engagement that runs in parallel with Phase 0 operations, not before
Why now

The pressure is increasing

  • Food affordability is worsening — 35.4% food inflation in 2024 is not a correction, it is a trend
  • Energy costs make inefficient movement more expensive every year
  • Businesses need more reliable sourcing as formal retail and food service expand in Lagos
  • Climate volatility makes supply unpredictability worse, not better

The conditions are ready

  • Better data tools and lightweight software make network coordination more feasible at small scale
  • Direct farm-to-buyer relationships are increasingly viable without large intermediary infrastructure
  • DFIs and impact investors are actively looking for evidence-backed models — not speculative cold room assets
  • Lagos's growing B2B food service sector creates an anchor demand pool that did not exist a decade ago

TRACE is not starting by building the largest cold room. It is starting by learning where the food system loses value — then building the assets, data systems, and infrastructure that remove those losses.

Capital philosophy

TRACE does not raise capital to build infrastructure.
TRACE raises capital to remove proven bottlenecks.

Five capital principles

  • Build demand before assets. A recurring B2B customer base is worth more than a cold room with no committed tenants.
  • Maximise learning before scale. Phase 0 exists to generate evidence, not revenue. The learning justifies Phase 1.
  • Deploy infrastructure only where utilisation is demonstrated. The hub gets built where data says it should be, not where assumptions suggest.
  • Use renewable energy where it lowers operating cost and improves reliability. Solar is not a brand statement — it is a cost and FX hedge.
  • Prefer operational evidence over market-size assumptions. The DFI conversation happens with data, not a deck.

Long-term economic moat

TRACE's competitive advantage is not owning trucks or cold rooms. It is owning the operational intelligence generated by coordinating food movement at scale.

How the moat compounds

  • As the network grows, every transaction improves supplier selection, demand forecasting, pricing, routing, grading, inventory allocation, and future infrastructure placement
  • Data from Phase 0 makes Phase 1 decisions cheaper and more accurate
  • Data from Phase 1 makes Phase 2 infrastructure more productive than a competitor building without that history
  • The intelligence layer cannot be replicated by someone building a cold room without first operating the coordination network

The result

  • Physical assets become more productive because they are informed by superior data rather than assumptions

Solar-first design eliminates largest naira cost. 30–40% of revenue USD-indexed (export, RFID SaaS, AI grading). Natural hedge: naira opex falls in dollar terms when naira weakens.
Critical
Logistics Theft & Hijacking
Armed robbery of refrigerated trucks on Nigerian highways is documented and recurring on Lagos–Ibadan, Kano–Kaduna, and Southeast routes.
What could go wrong
A single hijacking event destroys perishable cargo, the truck, and potentially a driver. A kidnapped driver is both a human tragedy and a business-ending reputational event.
Mitigation
Route risk assessment, curfew protocols, no night driving in high-risk corridors. Real-time GPS geofencing. Armed escort for pharma loads. K&R insurance for drivers on rural routes.
Critical
Power Infrastructure Failure
In 38–42°C ambient, a 4-hour power failure in a cold room without backup is a total produce loss event.
What could go wrong
Inverter faults and battery degradation create temperature excursions. A pharmaceutical excursion is a product liability event with NAFDAC consequences.
Mitigation
Redundant design: solar primary, battery secondary, diesel tertiary. Auto transfer switch <30 second failover. 24/7 monitoring, SMS alerts. Maintenance SLA with refrigeration OEM from day one.
High risks — material impact on timeline or returns
High
Hub Underutilisation
An LOI is not a volume commitment. Seasonal businesses over-commit and under-use without penalty if the contract is poorly structured.
What could go wrong
Brazil cold chain plays burned fixed costs for years at 30–40% utilisation. Hub fixed costs don't flex with seasonal demand dips.
Mitigation
Minimum monthly volume clause in every SLA — not a capacity reservation, a contracted floor. Tiered pricing rewards consistent commitment. Phase 0 logistics provides a cash floor.
High
Regulatory & Licensing Delays
NAFDAC pharma accreditation, NERC surplus licence, Lagos State planning consent can each take 12–24 months.
What could go wrong
Sequential filing (not parallel) creates 36+ month delay. Informal facilitation demands raise cost and legal exposure beyond the 12% contingency.
Mitigation
Engage regulatory counsel before site selection. File all licences in parallel. Designate government relations lead from day one. NIRSAL partnership accelerates federal alignment.
High
Talent Scarcity — Cold Chain Technicians
Nigeria has a critical shortage of trained refrigeration engineers. A compressor failure with no technician within 4 hours is a product loss event.
What could go wrong
Hub uptime depends on rapid fault diagnosis. Reactive repair frequency is high without preventive maintenance — and the talent to run it simply doesn't exist in sufficient supply.
Mitigation
Partner with refrigeration OEM for training and certification programme. Retain two trained technicians per hub. Preventive maintenance schedule to reduce reactive call-outs.
High
Banditry & Kidnapping on Rural Routes
Farm collection routes into Benue, Plateau, Kebbi, Kogi pass through areas with active banditry, particularly in NW and Northcentral zones.
What could go wrong
Initial hub siting in Lagos and Kano minimises exposure to highest-risk zones, but farm collection routes cannot always avoid them.
Mitigation
Farm collection limited to zones with state security or community protection. Driver journey management with mandatory check-ins. No solo driving in medium-risk zones. K&R insurance.
Medium risks — manageable with early action
Medium
Behavioural Adoption Lag
Smallholder farmers and traders have adapted to spoilage as accepted loss. Changing behaviour takes 3–5× longer than projected — validated by ColdHubs field data.
What could go wrong
Cooperative and farmer segments ramp slower than institutional tiers. If modelled as anchor revenue in Year 1, the shortfall creates an EBITDA gap.
Mitigation
Do not model farmer/cooperative volumes as anchor revenue in Year 1. Institutional B2B carries the hub economics. Farmer adoption is the social impact story and Year 3+ revenue uplift.
Medium
Solar Degradation & Battery Replacement
In Nigerian heat and harmattan dust, lithium cells degrade faster than temperate benchmarks. Year 4–6 battery reinvestment may be a capital event the model understates.
What could go wrong
The 5% O&M assumption may understate Year 4–6 battery replacement cost. A capacity shortfall at year five is a surprise CapEx event.
Mitigation
Annual battery capacity testing from Year 2. Battery replacement reserve as sinking fund from Year 3. Tropical climate battery ratings specified. 10-year EPC performance warranty.

07 — Operating Model

An operations company
disguised as a produce company.

The produce is what customers see. The real product is the system behind it — a set of repeatable processes through which every kilogram is tracked, graded, moved, and sold, generating intelligence at each step.

Status: Live

Inventory principle
FEFO — First Expiring, First Out
Not FIFO. The oldest item is not always the next to expire. The item closest to its shelf-life end moves first, always.

Supplier quality score — reliability and consistency per farm per week
Recovery rate — share of lower-grade produce redirected rather than discarded
Operating principle
TRACE will not rely on refrigeration alone to protect freshness. Quality is created through disciplined buying, receiving, grading, storage, routing and daily inventory decisions — not by building a bigger cold room.

Cross-cutting operating principles
👥
People
Hire operators before managers. Someone who has done the job before someone who has managed it.
Cross-train staff across at least two roles. No single point of failure on the floor.
Build SOP-driven execution. Consistency comes from process, not individual skill.
📋
SOPs
Every recurring process is documented before the second time it runs.
New hires are trained against written standards — not verbal instructions.
Continuous improvement: every SOP has a version number and a review date.
📦
Demand Planning
Forecast by SKU and location — not in aggregate.
Use B2B standing orders to anchor procurement. Know 70% of next week's demand before Monday.
Let demand drive purchasing — not intuition, relationship, or habit.

Waste Management
Measure waste at every handoff — farm, transit, receiving, floor, and cascade.
Track root causes: is spoilage happening at the farm, in transit, or on the floor? The answer changes the intervention.
Redirect near-expiry product to frozen, prepared, or secondary channels before it becomes waste.

Every batch is traceable back to the farm and harvest date.
Every week produces operational learnings that improve the system. Weekly review is non-negotiable.

Design principles

Building envelope & insulation

  • 120mm PIR insulated panel minimum — Nigeria tropical spec (100mm is temperate standard, insufficient at 42°C ambient differential)
  • Long axis runs E–W: long north/south walls minimise afternoon solar gain on primary storage wall faces
  • Reflective white TPO/PVC roof membrane reduces radiant heat load by 25–35% vs dark roof at Lagos solar irradiance
  • Vapour barrier on the warm face of every panel — critical at Lagos humidity 60–90% RH, harmattan dust sealing also required
  • Raised heated floor slab in frozen zone — prevents ground heave from sustained −18°C soil temperature
  • Zero windows in any cold zone. Every zone transition has a double-door airlock vestibule — reduces heat ingress by 60%+ vs direct-access doors

Refrigeration & energy systems

  • Variable-speed compressors throughout — 30–40% energy saving at part-load vs fixed-speed (critical: hub rarely runs at full load in early years)
  • Distributed evaporator coils in every zone — no single point of failure; any coil can be isolated without zone shutdown
  • Ammonia/HFC hybrid: ammonia primary plant room, HFC in GDP pharma zone (ammonia excluded from pharma for regulatory compliance)
  • Waste heat from compressors routed to pre-cooling vestibule and hot water for welfare facilities — heat recovery reduces OPEX
  • LEDs on motion sensors only inside cold zones — ‘dark warehouse’ design reduces internal heat gain and electricity cost
  • Battery room climate-controlled at +20°C constant — extends lithium cell cycle life by 40%+ vs ambient storage in Lagos heat

Material flow & movement of goods

  • One-way truck flow west→east across north apron — eliminates reversing conflicts at dock, reduces accident risk
  • Produce enters south face → processing → cold storage (flows north) — linear flow with no product crossing pathways
  • Separate dock levellers and airlocks per temperature zone — prevents zone contamination and temperature bleed between zones
  • 3.6m minimum aisle width throughout — reach truck plus passing clearance, meets AS/RS automation retrofit specification
  • Drive-in racking: 3 pallets deep, 5 high — FIFO with depth lanes, designed for AS/RS retrofit from Phase 2 without racking replacement
  • Fixed UHF RFID reader at every dock gate and every zone transition — chain-of-custody recorded at each physical handoff point

Expansion & future-proofing

  • Phase 2 expansion zone reserved west of building — 50m × 40m = 2,000 sqm connects directly to existing cool and processing zones via new airlock panels
  • Plant room oversized 20% at build — spare compressor slots and inverter capacity pre-installed; no plant room expansion required for Phase 2
  • Battery room conduit pre-run to western expansion boundary during original construction — saves significant retrofit cost
  • AS/RS automation pre-designed: floor loading at 6 T/sqm and 3.6m aisle width meet full automation requirements throughout
  • Modular cold room panel construction — any zone can be subdivided, enlarged, or re-temperatured at relatively low cost
  • NERC surplus energy connection sized for combined Phase 1+2 load from day one — no metering upgrade at Phase 2

Corridor logic — network economics layer (coming next)

The hub site is a node, not an isolated facility. The economic value of each location is determined by the density of produce flows, retail demand, and pharma distribution that pass through its catchment corridor. This analysis sits between Phase 0 route data and Phase 1 site commitment — it is the layer that turns a good location into a network decision.

Target Corridors — Lagos Hub 1

  • Lagos → Ibadan — highest produce volume corridor in SW Nigeria. Agro-processor cluster, Bodija market proximity.
  • Lagos → Abeokuta — Ogun State produce aggregation, FMCG manufacturing corridor (Promasidor, Honeywell).
  • Lagos → Benin — Midwest produce origin, Mid-West FMCG distribution corridor.
  • Lagos → Port Harcourt — Southern produce and pharma distribution. Highest pharmaceutical density outside Lagos.

What the Corridor Analysis Will Produce

  • Retail density — supermarket, FMCG, and food service concentration per corridor
  • Produce density — farm aggregation volumes, seasonal peaks, commodity mix per corridor
  • Pharma density — pharmaceutical distributor network, hospital proximity, cold chain gaps

07 — Risk Register

An honest account of
what can go wrong.

Ratings reflect inherent risk before mitigation. Review quarterly.

Source: Vision_Refined.pdf
Last updated: 2026-05-17
Status: Core
The cofounder gap is the single biggest execution risk. Every failure mode in this plan traces back to having the wrong person — or no person — in the operations seat. A visionary founder with the right thesis and no operational cofounder is the most common failure mode in African infrastructure plays. The physical infrastructure works. The model works. The question is who is running it.
Critical risks — threaten business viability
Critical
Cofounder Gap
Every assumption in this plan is a single point of failure until the operations seat is filled by someone who has run physical infrastructure under pressure in Nigeria.
What could go wrong
Visionary founder without operational cofounder — the most common failure mode in African infrastructure plays. Phase 0 trucks start but no one is accountable for compressor failures, driver non-compliance, or SLA breaches.
Mitigation
Non-negotiable: no Phase 1 capital deployment without cofounder in seat. Use Phase 0 truck programme as the live test — cofounder should be running it, not observing it. See Cofounder Brief.
Critical
FX & Currency Volatility
Naira has lost 70%+ against USD in three years. DFI debt, equipment replacement, and investor IRR all USD-denominated.
What could go wrong
A sharp devaluation widens the gap between naira revenues and dollar obligations faster than the model can absorb. FX escalation at +₦100/year may be insufficient in a crisis scenario.
Mitigation
Solar-first design eliminates largest naira cost. 30–40% of revenue USD-indexed (export, RFID SaaS, AI grading). Natural hedge: naira opex falls in dollar terms when naira weakens.
Critical
Logistics Theft & Hijacking
Armed robbery of refrigerated trucks on Nigerian highways is documented and recurring on Lagos–Ibadan, Kano–Kaduna, and Southeast routes.
What could go wrong
A single hijacking event destroys perishable cargo, the truck, and potentially a driver. A kidnapped driver is both a human tragedy and a business-ending reputational event.
Mitigation
Route risk assessment, curfew protocols, no night driving in high-risk corridors. Real-time GPS geofencing. Armed escort for pharma loads. K&R insurance for drivers on rural routes.
Critical
Power Infrastructure Failure
In 38–42°C ambient, a 4-hour power failure in a cold room without backup is a total produce loss event.
What could go wrong
Inverter faults and battery degradation create temperature excursions. A pharmaceutical excursion is a product liability event with NAFDAC consequences.
Mitigation
Redundant design: solar primary, battery secondary, diesel tertiary. Auto transfer switch <30 second failover. 24/7 monitoring, SMS alerts. Maintenance SLA with refrigeration OEM from day one.
High risks — material impact on timeline or returns
High
Hub Underutilisation
An LOI is not a volume commitment. Seasonal businesses over-commit and under-use without penalty if the contract is poorly structured.
What could go wrong
Brazil cold chain plays burned fixed costs for years at 30–40% utilisation. Hub fixed costs don't flex with seasonal demand dips.
Mitigation
Minimum monthly volume clause in every SLA — not a capacity reservation, a contracted floor. Tiered pricing rewards consistent commitment. Phase 0 logistics provides a cash floor.
High
Regulatory & Licensing Delays
NAFDAC pharma accreditation, NERC surplus licence, Lagos State planning consent can each take 12–24 months.
What could go wrong
Sequential filing (not parallel) creates 36+ month delay. Informal facilitation demands raise cost and legal exposure beyond the 12% contingency.
Mitigation
Engage regulatory counsel before site selection. File all licences in parallel. Designate government relations lead from day one. NIRSAL partnership accelerates federal alignment.
High
Talent Scarcity — Cold Chain Technicians
Nigeria has a critical shortage of trained refrigeration engineers. A compressor failure with no technician within 4 hours is a product loss event.
What could go wrong
Hub uptime depends on rapid fault diagnosis. Reactive repair frequency is high without preventive maintenance — and the talent to run it simply doesn't exist in sufficient supply.
Mitigation
Partner with refrigeration OEM for training and certification programme. Retain two trained technicians per hub. Preventive maintenance schedule to reduce reactive call-outs.
High
Banditry & Kidnapping on Rural Routes
Farm collection routes into Benue, Plateau, Kebbi, Kogi pass through areas with active banditry, particularly in NW and Northcentral zones.
What could go wrong
Initial hub siting in Lagos and Kano minimises exposure to highest-risk zones, but farm collection routes cannot always avoid them.
Mitigation
Farm collection limited to zones with state security or community protection. Driver journey management with mandatory check-ins. No solo driving in medium-risk zones. K&R insurance.
Medium risks — manageable with early action
Medium
Behavioural Adoption Lag
Smallholder farmers and traders have adapted to spoilage as accepted loss. Changing behaviour takes 3–5× longer than projected — validated by ColdHubs field data.
What could go wrong
Cooperative and farmer segments ramp slower than institutional tiers. If modelled as anchor revenue in Year 1, the shortfall creates an EBITDA gap.
Mitigation
Do not model farmer/cooperative volumes as anchor revenue in Year 1. Institutional B2B carries the hub economics. Farmer adoption is the social impact story and Year 3+ revenue uplift.
Medium
Solar Degradation & Battery Replacement
In Nigerian heat and harmattan dust, lithium cells degrade faster than temperate benchmarks. Year 4–6 battery reinvestment may be a capital event the model understates.
What could go wrong
The 5% O&M assumption may understate Year 4–6 battery replacement cost. A capacity shortfall at year five is a surprise CapEx event.
Mitigation
Annual battery capacity testing from Year 2. Battery replacement reserve as sinking fund from Year 3. Tropical climate battery ratings specified. 10-year EPC performance warranty.
